# Narrative Identity Seat Notes

## Evidence status

This seat contains 20 counted, unique journal papers: 5 inspected at full-text depth and 15 at abstract depth. All records were independently cross-reviewed by psychometrics (`reviewer=019f4f06-6d72-79f1-9cdd-3d4b6163199b`, `reviewed_at=2026-07-11T04:10:47Z`). The work used only public paper sources. No WhatsApp content, private profile, local database, or credential was accessed.

The bounded question was how a digital brain should represent autobiographical organization, self-defining memories, life stories, autobiographical reasoning, continuity and change, reconstruction, life periods, and owner elicitation. It was not a review of generic machine long-term memory or a license to infer clinical state.

## Search strategy

Searches combined exact-title verification with concept queries on PubMed/PMC and publisher or DOI pages. Query families included:

- `autobiographical memory organization lifetime periods life story chapters`
- `self-defining memories specificity meaning content affect`
- `life narrative temporal causal thematic coherence development`
- `narrative identity continuity change repeated narratives longitudinal`
- `autobiographical reasoning turning points growth agency`
- `self-concept change reconstructive autobiographical memory perspective`
- `I am statements self images autobiographical memory elicitation`

The organization search yielded hierarchical cueing and life-period studies (`paper:conway-bekerian-1987-organization`, `paper:thomsen-berntsen-2008-life-script-chapters`, `paper:chen-mcanally-reese-2013-memory-organization`). Self-image and identity-strength searches added `paper:rathbone-moulin-conway-2008-self-centered` and `paper:haslam-et-al-2011-self-knowledge-identity`. Narrative-development and longitudinal searches supplied the remaining records.

For every included paper, title, authors, year, venue, DOI, and PubMed identifier were checked against a primary record. Published versions were preferred and no preprint/published pair was counted twice. Full text was inspected where an official publisher PDF or PMC article was available; otherwise the record is explicitly abstract-depth.

## Inclusion and exclusion

Included papers had to be a primary journal paper with a stable identifier, a primary HTTPS page, an inspectable method or contribution, and a direct bearing on this seat. The set includes experiments, longitudinal studies, cohort-sequential studies, qualitative longitudinal analysis, and a coding-method paper with an original 498-narrative application (`paper:reese-et-al-2011-narrative-coherence`).

Excluded items included books and chapters, pure reviews without an original empirical or methodological contribution, duplicate versions, blogs, product pages, repository documentation, secondary explainers, unverifiable citations, generic episodic-memory engineering, and work whose main claim required clinical diagnosis. Papers were also omitted when the primary source did not support a sufficiently specific finding or identifier.

## Evidence-backed synthesis

### Validated

**Autobiographical retrieval is personally indexed and can use layered cues.** Personal lifetime-period primes facilitated retrieval where generic semantic primes did not, but the study could not distinguish a stored hierarchy from cue-driven construction of retrieval plans (`paper:conway-bekerian-1987-organization`). Life chapters and their boundaries organize selected memories, while cultural scripts also shape which boundaries are salient (`paper:thomsen-berntsen-2008-life-script-chapters`). The ability to organize episodes under lifetime periods develops through childhood and adolescence, while amount and specificity of narration vary across cultural groups (`paper:chen-mcanally-reese-2013-memory-organization`). The evidence supports exposing owner-defined period and general-event cue paths; it does not establish one necessary underlying storage topology.

**Self-images are useful retrieval cues, not timeless trait truth.** Memories clustered around the reported formation of owner-generated `I am` identities, especially concrete roles (`paper:rathbone-moulin-conway-2008-self-centered`). Personal semantic knowledge and episodic recollection both related to identity strength, but the supported direction was bidirectional rather than settled (`paper:haslam-et-al-2011-self-knowledge-identity`). A self-image should be a versioned retrieval node with evidence, formation estimates, and contradictions.

**A self-defining memory has multiple independent properties.** Specificity, meaning, content, and affect were separately codeable and related differently to adjustment measures (`paper:blagov-singer-2004-four-dimensions`). Relationship memories also differed by parent or peer context, and conflict could coexist with closeness and meaning (`paper:mclean-thorne-2003-relationship-memories`). The architecture should not compress event, interpretation, affect, and relational setting into one importance score.

**Narrative identity is socially told and developmentally constructed.** Reported audience shifted from parents toward peers across late adolescence, while self-explanation elicited more meaning than entertainment (`paper:mclean-2005-meaning-telling`). Meaning in turning-point stories related to identity measures, but its form varied by story type (`paper:mclean-pratt-2006-turning-points`). Audience, telling purpose, and current interpretation therefore belong in provenance, not in the event's supposedly objective facts.

**Coherence is multidimensional, not a universal quality scalar.** Context, chronology, and theme have distinguishable developmental requirements (`paper:reese-et-al-2011-narrative-coherence`). Whole-life temporal, causal-motivational, and thematic coherence also change on different schedules (`paper:habermas-de-silveira-2008-global-coherence`, `paper:kober-schmiedek-habermas-2015-coherence-development`). The model should retain each dimension and the underlying narrative instead of silently polishing an account into one globally coherent story.

**Continuity includes both recurrence and revision.** Repeated life-story scenes showed stable features alongside developmental changes (`paper:mcadams-et-al-2006-continuity-change`). Across eight years, life-narrative segments were more stable than individually selected important memories, and normative transitions were repeated more often (`paper:kober-habermas-2017-personal-past-stability`). Repeated occupational stories also acquired new identity meanings (`paper:jardmo-et-al-2023-repeated-narratives`). Stable event identity and changing interpretation must therefore be stored separately.

**Recall is reconstructive and elicitation can alter the working self.** Current self-compatibility affected whether memories were reconstructed from first- or third-person perspective (`paper:libby-eibach-2002-looking-back`). Prompting past or future self-defining events shifted immediate self-description and temporal continuity (`paper:dargembeau-garcia-jimenez-2024-working-self`). An interview response is evidence produced in a context, not a context-free readout of a fixed identity.

### Plausible

A defensible digital-brain design is a versioned autobiographical graph with nodes for `life_period`, `general_event`, `episode`, `self_image`, and `narrative_version`, plus edges for owner-stated meaning, relationship scope, audience, affect-at-recall, and temporal continuity. This structure is a design inference from the layered, self-cued, and longitudinal evidence above; no included paper directly evaluates this software architecture.

It is also plausible that owner-generated chapters and self-images can improve retrieval coverage compared with generic keyword interviews (`paper:conway-bekerian-1987-organization`, `paper:rathbone-moulin-conway-2008-self-centered`). The prompts must remain optional and editable because cultural scripts, current goals, and the prompt itself can shape the result (`paper:thomsen-berntsen-2008-life-script-chapters`, `paper:dargembeau-garcia-jimenez-2024-working-self`).

### Speculative

It remains speculative that narrative constructs extracted from messaging behavior can produce a faithful digital replica. None of these studies validates WhatsApp-derived narrative identity, LLM simulation fidelity, automatic life-period induction, or owner acceptance of generated interpretations. Those claims require separate consented evaluation, not extrapolation from this corpus.

## Negative and conflicting evidence

- Brief autobiographical-reasoning training and simply narrating twice did not reliably increase life-story coherence (`paper:habermas-de-silveira-2008-global-coherence`). More prompting is not automatically better elicitation.
- During psychotherapy, agency increased and preceded improvement, but coherence did not increase (`paper:adler-2012-living-into-story`). Coherence cannot stand in for adaptation or health.
- Semantic knowledge may mediate episodic memory's relation to identity, but the reverse mediation also fit (`paper:haslam-et-al-2011-self-knowledge-identity`). The system should not claim one causal direction.
- Differentiated reasoning about negative events related to well-being, while differentiated reasoning about positive events did not; socioeconomic status and event severity complicated the result (`paper:lilgendahl-mcadams-2011-self-growth`). Forced growth interpretation would overclaim and may be harmful.
- Exploratory processing and positive resolution followed different pathways (`paper:pals-2006-difficult-experiences`). Unresolved, negative, or ambivalent narratives are valid states, not failed records.
- Concrete self-images were more tightly dated than abstract ones (`paper:rathbone-moulin-conway-2008-self-centered`), and cultural groups differed in narrative amount and specificity (`paper:chen-mcanally-reese-2013-memory-organization`). One confidence rule cannot be applied across identity types or owners.
- Longitudinal repetition measured stability, not factual correctness (`paper:kober-habermas-2017-personal-past-stability`), while current self-concept changed recall perspective (`paper:libby-eibach-2002-looking-back`). Repetition and confidence must not be treated as ground truth.

## Implications for a digital brain

1. **Use a layered autobiographical store.** Link episodes to general events, owner-defined periods, and life chapters. Permit overlap because periods such as relationships, locations, education, and work can coexist (`paper:conway-bekerian-1987-organization`, `paper:chen-mcanally-reese-2013-memory-organization`).
2. **Separate event from interpretation.** Keep source claims, dates, participants, and uncertainty distinct from later meaning, affect, agency, and perspective. Append a new narrative version instead of overwriting (`paper:mcadams-et-al-2006-continuity-change`, `paper:jardmo-et-al-2023-repeated-narratives`).
3. **Make self-images owner-authored.** Use `I am` prompts as optional retrieval handles, record estimated formation and retirement periods, and attach both supporting and disconfirming episodes (`paper:rathbone-moulin-conway-2008-self-centered`).
4. **Preserve social context without profiling others.** Record audience and owner-stated relationship role, but do not infer contact traits or copy third-party claims into the owner model (`paper:mclean-thorne-2003-relationship-memories`, `paper:mclean-2005-meaning-telling`).
5. **Annotate coherence, never manufacture it.** Keep temporal, contextual, causal, and thematic annotations alongside the raw owner account; low coherence should trigger clarification or uncertainty, not automatic rewriting (`paper:reese-et-al-2011-narrative-coherence`, `paper:kober-schmiedek-habermas-2015-coherence-development`).
6. **Treat elicitation as a measured intervention.** Log the exact prompt, order, timestamp, and whether the response was spontaneous, recalled, inferred, or imagined. Ask for confirmation outside the immediate priming context when a claim will affect the durable profile (`paper:dargembeau-garcia-jimenez-2024-working-self`).
7. **Evaluate fidelity by retrieval and revision behavior.** Tests should ask whether the system finds the correct owner-defined period, distinguishes fact from meaning, retrieves competing versions, cites provenance, and expresses uncertainty. Narrative polish or inferred well-being is not a fidelity metric (`paper:adler-2012-living-into-story`).

## Owner elicitation design

A cautious elicitation sequence follows the evidence without assuming that the archive is complete:

1. Ask the owner to name current and former life chapters, allowing overlaps and a skip option (`paper:conway-bekerian-1987-organization`, `paper:thomsen-berntsen-2008-life-script-chapters`).
2. For each chosen chapter, ask for recurring situations before specific episodes; do not require equal detail across chapters (`paper:chen-mcanally-reese-2013-memory-organization`).
3. Invite a small set of owner-generated `I am` statements and ask when each became, ceased to be, or never was defining (`paper:rathbone-moulin-conway-2008-self-centered`).
4. For selected episodes, separately ask what happened, why it matters now, how it felt at recall, who has heard it, and whether the meaning has changed (`paper:blagov-singer-2004-four-dimensions`, `paper:mclean-2005-meaning-telling`).
5. Ask for counter-stories and exceptions before promoting a self-image or turning point to a durable claim (`paper:mclean-pratt-2006-turning-points`, `paper:libby-eibach-2002-looking-back`).
6. Make difficult experiences opt-in, permit withdrawal, and never demand positive resolution (`paper:pals-2006-difficult-experiences`, `paper:lilgendahl-mcadams-2011-self-growth`).

## WhatsApp observability gaps

No WhatsApp data was inspected. The following are constraints on any future, consented use of messaging records:

- Messages contain stories selected for a particular audience and purpose. They do not reveal untold memories, silent periods, or the owner's complete life story (`paper:mclean-2005-meaning-telling`).
- A message can contain an episode but usually does not establish the owner's life-period boundary, the identity's formation date, or whether the event is self-defining (`paper:conway-bekerian-1987-organization`, `paper:rathbone-moulin-conway-2008-self-centered`).
- Repeated tellings can reveal narrative versions, but repetition does not prove event accuracy and variation does not prove deception (`paper:kober-habermas-2017-personal-past-stability`, `paper:jardmo-et-al-2023-repeated-narratives`).
- Text cannot reliably expose first- versus third-person imagery, unexpressed affect, current self-compatibility, or the meaning the owner would endorse now (`paper:blagov-singer-2004-four-dimensions`, `paper:libby-eibach-2002-looking-back`).
- Message volume and detail are unsafe proxies for importance or identity completeness because narrative specificity varies with age, culture, audience, and cue (`paper:chen-mcanally-reese-2013-memory-organization`).
- Cultural milestones visible in conversation may help propose chapter prompts, but must not be converted into assumed life events (`paper:thomsen-berntsen-2008-life-script-chapters`).
- Claims involving another person are relationship-scoped evidence about the owner, not consent to model the other person's identity (`paper:mclean-thorne-2003-relationship-memories`).
- Future plans in chat may be aspirations, hypotheticals, jokes, or abandoned possibilities. They require owner confirmation and must remain distinct from memories (`paper:dargembeau-garcia-jimenez-2024-working-self`).

These gaps should become adaptive owner questions with explicit skip controls, not hidden psychological inference.

## Open questions

- What event-matching procedure can preserve one event identity across changed dates, details, and interpretations without collapsing genuinely different events?
- How often can chapters and self-images be re-elicited before the interview itself produces unacceptable profile drift?
- Which prompts recover neglected periods without importing a culturally normative life script?
- How should the model represent an owner who endorses two conflicting meanings of the same event in different relationships or periods?
- What minimum evidence should be required before a self-image moves from tentative to owner-confirmed, and how should retirement or revocation work?
- Can owner-visible narrative diffs distinguish factual correction, ordinary reconstruction, audience adaptation, and genuine identity change?
- How should third-party privacy limits constrain relationship memories that are central to the owner but disclose another person's sensitive history?
- Which evaluation tasks test narrative continuity without rewarding verbosity, positivity, adult-style coherence, or memorization of private text?
